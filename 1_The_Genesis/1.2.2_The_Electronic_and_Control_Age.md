# History of Automation: Part 2
## The Electronic & Control Age (1900 – 1990)

**Scope**: The formalization of PID control, the birth of industrial robotics, PLCs, and Computer Numerical Control (CNC).

---

## 1. Early 20th Century Innovations

### 1913: Henry Ford's Assembly Line
**Event**: Ford implements a moving assembly line for Model T production.  
**Metrics**: Assembly time reduced from 728 hours per car to 93 hours (87.5% efficiency gain).  
**Significance**: Mechanized motion combined with task specialization creates exponential productivity gains, proving mass manufacturing profitability and catalyzing worldwide adoption.

### 1922: Nicolas Minorsky's PID Controller Theory
**Event**: Minorsky publishes a mathematical analysis of ship steering control.  
**Key Insight**: Human helmsmen use three principles: Proportional (correct current error), Integral (accumulate past errors), and Derivative (anticipate future error).  
**Impact**: PID becomes the dominant control method for over 100 years. As of 2026, it still accounts for the vast majority of industrial controllers.

### 1943: Norbert Wiener's Cybernetics
**Event**: Rosenblueth, Wiener, and Bigelow publish "Behavior, Purpose and Teleology".  
**Thesis**: Feedback-driven goal-seeking behavior is a universal principle applying to animals, machines, and organizations.  
**Impact**: Establishes automation and systems engineering as a rigorous scientific discipline.

### 1949: John T. Parsons & First CNC Machine
**Event**: Working with MIT, Parsons develops a system to machine complex helicopter blade profiles by feeding coordinate data from punched cards into a servomotor-driven milling machine.
**Significance**: The birth of Computer Numerical Control (CNC). This transitioned machining from human-guided tracing of physical templates to pure mathematical, digital precision, laying the groundwork for modern subtractive manufacturing.

## 2. Early Industrial Robotics (1950 - 1980)

### 1954: George Devol's Unimate Robot
**Event**: George Devol designs the first programmable industrial robot arm, powered by hydraulics.  
**Technical Specs**: 6 degrees of freedom, punched tape memory, ±1 inch accuracy at a 5-foot reach.  
**Innovation**: Combines programmable memory (Jacquard loom principle) with electrical control and mechanical manipulation.

### 1961: First Industrial Robot Deployment (GM Plant)
**Event**: The Unimate robot is installed at the General Motors Ewing, New Jersey facility.  
**Application**: Casting part extraction and die-casting machine tending (a highly dangerous, repetitive task).  
**Impact**: Proves the commercial viability of industrial robots and marks the definitive birth of the robotics industry.

### 1968: Dick Morley's Programmable Logic Controller (PLC)
**Event**: Dick Morley designs the first PLC for Bedford Associates.  
**Problem Solved**: Hard-wired relay logic circuits were inflexible and required complete rewiring for any process changes.  
**Solution**: Replace physical relays with solid-state switching; program the logic in software using "ladder logic."  
**Impact**: Completely transforms industrial process control, enabling rapid reconfiguration of factory lines.

### 1973: 6-Axis Industrial Robot Arm
**Event**: Development of the first electrically-driven, 6-DOF robot arm with servo feedback control.  
**Advancement**: Moves the industry beyond hydraulics, enabling much finer control and lower maintenance.  
**Impact**: Leads to widespread deployment of welding and assembly robots in automotive manufacturing by KUKA, ABB, and FANUC.

---

## 3. Digital Control & Early Networking (1980 - 1990)

### 1983: SCADA Systems Emerge
**Event**: Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition (SCADA) systems become widespread for centralized monitoring.  
**Architecture**: Central control centers communicating via telecommunications to Remote Terminal Units (RTUs) at the edge.  
**Impact**: Enables the remote operation of geographically massive critical infrastructure (power grids, water treatment, pipelines).
